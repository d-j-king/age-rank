2c — New "Scene 3: The Solidarity Network" section (after Scene 2 block)
New
Each agent is embedded in a social graph whose edges represent
relationships—shared cohort, shared history, informal alliance.
A higher-ranked neighbor exerts upward lift; the force is asymmetric,
since those already surpassed offer nothing further. This is the
formal rendering of in-group solidarity: advantage that flows along
the lines people actually draw around themselves.

  lift_i = (λ / N−1) · Σ_{j~i} max(0, r_j − r_i)

Three graph topologies are available. Age-banded graphs pack edges
within cohorts—the homophily case. Small-world graphs add sparse
cross-cohort bridges. Random graphs serve as the null: uniform
connectivity, no age structure.
#3 γ citation corrected — Lazear 1989 primary, Lindbeck–Snower secondary explainer.html · γ term + references
3a — γ term description (second paragraph)
Before
Economists call this insider holdup
[Lindbeck & Snower 1988]: seniors withhold
cooperation, context, and access, making
the junior's contributions less legible to
the org. The formula captures the magnitude
of their leverage.
After
The tournament literature shows that large
rank-prize spreads create incentives to impede
rivals rather than outperform them [Lazear 1989]
—a senior threatened by a rising junior faces
this incentive directly. Withholding cooperation,
context, and access makes the junior's
contributions less legible to the org without
any overt act required. Economists label the
structural leverage this creates insider holdup
[Lindbeck & Snower 1988]—though their original
framing is the labor market, the within-firm
mechanism is the same.

vocab Seniority vocabulary note — "How it works" section index.html · after variable definitions
Inserted between the variable definitions paragraph and "Three forces act…"
New
On the word seniority. Many readers will reach for seniority to
name what this simulation models. We use rank for hierarchical position
and age for tenure, keeping the two as separate variables. Seniority
blurs them—a senior person both has more age and typically holds higher
rank—and the model is precisely about how the first drives the second.
Conflating them makes the mechanism invisible.
